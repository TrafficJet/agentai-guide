## What Is a Real Estate Commission in 2025?

A real estate commission is a percentage of the final sale price paid to the agents involved. Historically, the seller paid this fee at closing — covering both the listing agent and the buyer's agent.

There is no legally required rate. Commissions have always been negotiable. The FTC made this point directly in its 2023 review of real estate industry competition (Source: FTC, 2023). Nationally, rates typically fall between 4.5% and 6%, but local markets vary.

The biggest change in 2025 comes from the NAR settlement that took effect August 17, 2024. Under the new rules, buyer agent compensation is negotiated separately from the listing agreement. Buyers must sign a written representation agreement disclosing their agent's fee before touring any home. Sellers no longer automatically bundle the buyer agent fee into the listing — they decide whether to offer it and how much.

**Real-world example:** Jane, a seller in Austin, TX, listed her home for $410,000 in early 2025. She negotiated a 1.5% listing fee and offered 2.5% to buyer's agents. Total commission: 4% ($16,400) instead of the typical 5.5% ($22,550). That one negotiation saved her $6,150.

Many sellers don't realize how much room exists here. A 2024 Clever Real Estate survey found that 57% of sellers who asked for a lower rate actually got one.

## How the 2024 NAR Settlement Changed Commissions

In March 2024, the National Association of Realtors agreed to a $418 million settlement to resolve antitrust lawsuits claiming the industry had artificially held commission rates high (Source: NAR, 2024). New rules took effect August 17, 2024.

### The Key MLS Rule Change

Listing agents can no longer advertise buyer agent compensation on the MLS — the shared database where agents post property listings. Before the settlement, an MLS listing might show "2.5% offered to buyer's agent." That field is gone. Buyer agent fees are now negotiated directly between the parties, outside the MLS.

### What Sellers Need to Know

You are not required to offer buyer agent compensation when you list. Many sellers still do — but it's now an explicit choice, not a default. This matters when you're [calculating your net proceeds](/seller-net-proceeds-calculator) because the buyer agent fee no longer comes pre-baked into the standard listing contract.

### What Buyers Need to Know

Before touring any home, you must sign a buyer representation agreement with your agent. It must clearly state how much your agent will be paid. That fee can come from the seller, from you directly, or some combination.

In practice, many sellers still offer buyer agent incentives to keep their buyer pool large. A NAR Member Survey from Q1 2025 found that roughly 70% of closed transactions still included seller-paid buyer agent compensation (Source: [NAR](https://www.nar.realtor), 2025). The settlement doesn't automatically save you money — but it gives you more control over the conversation.

## Tips to Negotiate a Lower Commission in 2025

### Interview Multiple Listing Agents

Get written proposals from at least three agents. Compare the rates, but also compare what's included — professional photography, staging consultation, open houses, marketing reach. Sellers who do this often find a 0.5–1% spread between proposals on the same property.

### Consider Discount Brokerages

Redfin charges a 1% listing fee in select markets as of 2025. Flat-fee MLS companies can cut your listing costs further. The tradeoff: [discount brokers](/best-discount-real-estate-brokers) often provide fewer hands-on services — less in-person availability, standardized marketing instead of custom strategies.

### Offer a Competitive Buyer Agent Fee

Even if you negotiate your listing side down, offering 2.5% or more to buyer's agents can bring in stronger buyer traffic. Cutting the buyer agent fee too hard — say, to 1% or less — may reduce showings and hurt your final price. A 2023 study in the *Journal of Financial Economics* found that homes offering lower buyer agent commissions took longer to sell and closed at lower prices relative to comparable listings.

### Use Market Conditions as Leverage

In a hot seller's market where homes sell in under two weeks, your agent spends less time and money on marketing. That's a fair reason to ask for a lower rate. In a slow market, an agent may push back — and reasonably so, since they'll spend more on staging, marketing, and extended showing schedules.

### Ask About Dual-Agency Discounts

If your listing agent also brings the buyer, you can often negotiate a lower total commission since both sides go to one brokerage. Dual agency has different legal rules by state. Eight states, including Florida and Colorado, restrict or prohibit it entirely (Source: National Conference of State Legislatures). Read the contract carefully before agreeing.

## For Sale By Owner (FSBO): Do You Still Pay Commission?

Selling FSBO eliminates the listing agent fee — typically 2.5–3% of the sale price. On a $400,000 home, that's $10,000–$12,000 you keep. Our [FSBO guide](/fsbo-guide) covers the full process.

After the NAR settlement, FSBO sellers decide for themselves whether to offer buyer agent compensation. There's no obligation to pay a buyer's agent. But roughly 88% of buyers used an agent in 2024 (Source: NAR Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, 2024). Refusing to compensate buyer agents may shrink your buyer pool significantly.

### FSBO Tradeoffs Are Real

The risks are measurable: pricing errors, legal exposure from disclosure mistakes, fewer showings. FSBO homes in 2024 had a median sale price of $380,000 versus $435,000 for agent-assisted sales — a $55,000 gap (Source: NAR, 2024). That difference can easily wipe out the commission savings, especially for sellers without real estate experience.

**A practical middle ground:** Pay $300–$500 for a flat-fee MLS listing to get on the Multiple Listing Service, then negotiate buyer agent compensation separately. You get MLS exposure without paying a full listing percentage. Services like Houzeo and Beycome offer flat-fee packages in most states as of 2025.

## How Agents Split Commission With Their Brokers

The commission you pay doesn't go entirely to the agent. Every licensed agent works under a brokerage, and the brokerage takes a cut — often a large one.

New agents typically split 50/50 with their brokerage. On a 2.75% buyer agent commission for a $400,000 sale ($11,000), the agent keeps $5,500 and the brokerage keeps $5,500. Experienced agents negotiate better terms — 70/30 or 80/20 splits are common for agents with strong track records (Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics / NAR, 2024).

Top producers sometimes work at flat-fee brokerages like eXp Realty or Real Broker, keeping 90–100% of the commission after paying a small per-deal fee — typically $500–$750. This structure explains why agents resist deep discounts. A 1% commission cut can mean a 30–50% drop in their personal take-home after the brokerage split, self-employment taxes, and marketing costs.

That doesn't mean you shouldn't negotiate. It means negotiate with clear eyes — know what you're asking for and what you expect in return. Our guide on [how to find a real estate agent](/how-to-find-a-real-estate-agent) walks through evaluating agents on value, not just price.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the average real estate commission rate in 2025?
Nationally, it falls between 4.5% and 6%, split between listing and buyer agents (Source: RealTrends Verified, 2025). Since the 2024 NAR settlement, negotiation is more open, and some sellers are landing closer to 4–5% total.

### Who pays the real estate commission — buyer or seller?
Traditionally, the seller pays both agents from the sale proceeds. After the August 2024 NAR rule changes, buyers may now pay their own agent directly. But roughly 70% of transactions in early 2025 still included seller-paid buyer agent compensation (Source: NAR Member Survey, Q1 2025).

### How do I calculate how much commission I'll owe?
Multiply your expected sale price by the agreed commission rate. A $350,000 home at 5% equals $17,500 total. Use the calculator above to see the split and your estimated net proceeds.

### Is real estate commission negotiable in 2025?
Yes. It has always been negotiable by law (Source: FTC, 2023). The 2024 NAR settlement made the process more transparent. You can compare agents, ask for lower rates, or use a discount broker to reduce costs.

### What changed about buyer agent commissions after the NAR settlement?
As of August 17, 2024, agents can no longer advertise buyer agent compensation on MLS listings. Sellers decide separately whether to offer it. Buyers must agree in writing to their agent's fee before touring homes (Source: NAR, 2024).

### Do I pay commission if my home doesn't sell?
In most listing agreements, no. But read your contract carefully. Some include cancellation fees or clauses that trigger a commission — for example, if you sell privately to a buyer the agent previously introduced.

### Can a seller refuse to pay the buyer's agent commission?
Yes. Under the post-settlement rules, sellers have no obligation to offer buyer agent compensation. But declining may reduce the number of buyers willing to tour your home, especially those who can't afford to pay their agent out of pocket.
